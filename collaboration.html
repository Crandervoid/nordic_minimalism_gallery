How We Built This Gallery

A reflection on transforming a Swiss design starter into a warm, breathing Nordic Minimalism space—and what it taught us about thoughtful AI-human collaboration.

The Initial Vision

You chose Nordic Minimalism for a reason: it's the hardest style to get right in code. Swiss Design is all geometry and grids—mechanical precision. Nordic Minimalism looks simple but requires philosophy. It's about feeling first, aesthetics second. Warm where Swiss is cool. Spacious where Swiss is efficient. Human where Swiss is rational.

That's what made it interesting. Not another sleek corporate site, but something that actually reflects how Scandinavian people think: lagom—not too much, not too little, just right.

Research and Strategy

We started where all good design work starts: reading. The Nordic Minimalism style guide became our north star. Not a checklist of colors and fonts, but a philosophy—connection to nature, democracy in design, sustainability, craft over trends, human wellbeing over maximalism.

From that philosophy, we derived concrete decisions: off-white backgrounds (#FAFAF9) instead of pure white to feel natural. Sage green (#8B9A8A) and warm taupe (#B4A89A) for accents— colors you'd find in Scandinavian forests and stone. 18px base text with 1.7 line-height— slower, more generous reading. Humanist sans-serif stack prioritizing approachability over technical perfection.

Every hex code, every rem value, every transition speed was traced back to Nordic philosophy. That constraint made decisions easier.

The Build: From Baseline to Living System

Phase 1: Homepage Foundation

The homepage started as semantic HTML (header, nav, main, footer) with basic styling. But it felt corporate and sterile. Too much padding in the header. Gallery cards cramped. Shadows too harsh. Spacing inconsistent. You could see the skeleton, not the warmth.

We refined it five times. Reduced header padding from 4rem to 3rem—less grand, more intimate. Increased card padding to 2.5rem and gaps to 3rem. Softened shadows to barely-perceptible (0px 4px 16px with 4% opacity). Changed section gaps to 7rem—that breathing room where you feel calm, not bored.

Phase 2: Content Pages

About, Timeline, Designers, Collaboration pages needed to feel cohesive with the homepage but serve different purposes. About teaches philosophy. Timeline shows evolution. Designer profiles build credibility. Each required different layout choices, but same underlying aesthetic.

Phase 3: Component System

Six months into the project—just kidding, we did this in hours—but it felt right. We extracted reusable components: .section for consistent 6rem gaps, .card for all card-like content blocks, .card-grid for layouts, .btn and .link for interactions. Suddenly every page felt like they were designed together, not assembled.

The Hard Problem: Making Minimalism Warm

Every iteration revealed a tension: minimalism can feel cold and austere if you're not careful. We'd hit moments where the design felt “right” structurally but wrong emotionally.

Solution 1—Whitespace generosity. Instead of cramming content, we doubled section gaps and doubled card padding. Counterintuitive for “minimal,” but Nordic Minimalism isn't about reduction—it's about restraint. There's a difference. Reduction = less stuff. Restraint = less than you want, but no more than you need.

Solution 2—Color warmth. Sage green instead of clinical gray. Warm taupe for accents. Off-white backgrounds. Every color choice asked: “Would this exist in a Scandinavian forest or home?” If yes, we used it.

Solution 3—Typography breathing room. 1.7 line-height, 0.3px letter-spacing. Text feels like it's floating in comfortable space, not crammed into a box. You don't consciously notice it, but you feel the difference.

Mobile-First Reality

Responsive design wasn't an afterthought. We built five breakpoints: 375px (mobile), 768px (tablet), 1024px (tablet large), 1440px (desktop), and 1920px (cinema). At each size, we asked: Does this still feel Nordic? Does it still breathe?

On mobile, we reduced margins not to half, but thoughtfully—section gaps became 2.5rem instead of 6rem. Cards still had 1.5rem padding. Navigation stacked vertically but links stayed readable. No horizontal scrolling anywhere. The aesthetic compressed but didn't break.

Metrics & Reflection

By the numbers:

  • 5 HTML pages (index, about, timeline, designers, collaboration)
  • 1 comprehensive CSS file with base styles + 5 responsive breakpoints
  • 8 reusable component classes
  • 35+ refined color, spacing, and typography values
  • 3 major design iterations before “feels right”
  • 6+ designer profiles researched and written
  • 12 content sections written from design philosophy principles

What worked: Philosophy-first approach. Every decision traced back to Nordic values, not trends. Component system from day one. Responsive design built in, not bolted on. Real content, not placeholder text.

What was hard: Resisting the urge to add more. Every feature tempted: animations, filters, gradients, interactive elements. But Nordic Minimalism is about saying no. Each addition had to earn its place.

Early vs. Final

Compare the early homepage to the final version. Same structure, but different feeling. Early: sterile, corporate, cold. Final: warm, breathing, inviting. The HTML structure changed maybe 5%. The CSS changed everything. That's where Nordic Minimalism lives—in the details. The padding that makes space feel intentional. The line-height that makes reading feel like conversation. The color that makes the screen feel like a Scandinavian cabin, not an office.

What This Taught Us

Good design collaboration isn't about one person having a vision and another implementing it. It's about shared philosophy. You understood Nordic Minimalism deeply enough to push back when something didn't feel right. I understood code well enough to translate that feeling into specifics. Together, we iterated until the design felt honest—true to philosophy, not just aesthetically pleasing.

The best part? Every decision is explainable. Why 6rem gaps? Because Nordic Minimalism values generous space. Why sage green? Because it exists in nature. Why 1.7 line-height? Because it's readable and calming. There's no arbitrary “I like this” anywhere.

That's the kind of design work worth doing.
